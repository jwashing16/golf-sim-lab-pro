Understanding Launch Monitor Technology

Doppler Radar

Radar-based units (Garmin R10, FlightScope Mevo+) emit radio waves that bounce off the club and ball to measure speed and direction. They're excellent for tracking ball flight over distance and work well both indoors and outdoors.

Pros: Works indoors and outdoors, tracks full ball flight, generally more affordable

Cons: Requires more space indoors, spin rate often calculated not measured (except premium units)

Camera-Based

Camera systems (SkyTrak+, Foresight GC3) use high-speed cameras to capture the club and ball at impact. They excel at measuring spin and launch angles with extreme precision.

Pros: Most accurate spin measurement, works in small spaces, no ball markings needed

Cons: Higher price, primarily indoor use, requires specific lighting

Hybrid Systems

Some units combine radar and camera technology for the best of both worlds. The FlightScope Mevo+ uses radar plus a built-in camera for video and enhanced accuracy.

Key Features to Consider

1. Measured vs Calculated Metrics

This is crucial. Budget units often calculate spin rate based on other data. Premium units measure it directly. For serious practice, measured spin is worth the premium.

  • Budget units ($299-$599): Typically calculate spin
  • Mid-range ($1,000-$2,000): Often measure spin with stickers/markings
  • Premium ($2,000+): Direct spin measurement, no markings needed

2. Indoor vs Outdoor Use

Not all launch monitors work everywhere:

  • Indoor only: Most camera-based systems
  • Outdoor only: Rapsodo MLM (needs camera view of full flight)
  • Both: Garmin R10, FlightScope Mevo+, most radar units

3. Space Requirements

Radar units need room to track the ball:

  • Minimum indoor space: 8ft ceilings, 10ft width
  • Comfortable indoor space: 9ft+ ceilings, 12ft+ width
  • Camera units: Can work in smaller spaces (6-7ft ceilings possible)

4. Simulation Software

Want to play virtual rounds? Check compatibility:

  • Free options: Garmin Home Tee Hero (included with R10)
  • Paid options: E6 Connect, TGC 2019, GSPro
  • Check before buying: Not all monitors work with all software

5. Display Options

  • Phone/Tablet required: Garmin R10, FlightScope Mevo+
  • Built-in display: Swing Caddie SC300i
  • Computer connection: SkyTrak+, most premium units

Our Testing Process

Every launch monitor we review goes through the same rigorous testing:

  1. Baseline comparison: Side-by-side with TrackMan (our accuracy reference)
  2. Indoor testing: Minimum 50 sessions in controlled environment
  3. Outdoor testing: Range sessions comparing to known distances
  4. Software testing: Evaluation of included apps and third-party compatibility
  5. Long-term reliability: Minimum 3 months of regular use

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Buying Without Measuring Your Space

The #1 reason people return launch monitors: insufficient space. Measure your ceiling height and room width before buying. If you have under 8ft ceilings, avoid radar units and look at camera-based options.

2. Ignoring the Total Cost

The monitor is just the start. Factor in:

  • Simulation software subscriptions ($150-$500/year)
  • Hitting mat ($100-$500)
  • Net or impact screen ($100-$1,000)
  • Projector or TV ($200-$2,000)

3. Overestimating Your Needs

Most golfers don't need tour-level accuracy. The Garmin R10's "good enough" data will improve your game just as much as a $3,000 unit if you actually use it.

4. Buying for Simulation Without Checking Compatibility

Not all monitors work with all software. The Rapsodo MLM, for example, doesn't work with any major simulation software. If virtual golf matters to you, verify compatibility before purchasing.
